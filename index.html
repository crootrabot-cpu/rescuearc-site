The problem

Ring buoys are trusted — and still lousy projectiles.

Traditional throwable flotation is mature and regulation-friendly, but it is aerodynamically weak, accuracy-poor, and hard for average people to launch far under stress. That gap matters when every second of reach time counts.

  • Bulky geometry: ring forms are awkward to launch cleanly.
  • Low repeatability: average users do not throw them well.
  • Slow first contact: if the first throw misses, the rescue clock keeps burning.

Current category pain

Throwables are either clumsy, line-dependent, or tech-heavy.

Ring buoys are awkward. Throw bags require line handling and victim grip. Powered rescue devices are expensive. Compact inflatable rescue devices exist nearby, but none own the story of better throw mechanics for ordinary humans.

Proposed wedge

Make the first rescue throw more athletic, repeatable, and fast.

RescueArc reframes the category around launch performance first: football-like grip, cleaner spiral path, more attempts in a rack, then fast visible flotation after splash.

Hero scenario

The chair-side rack changes the whole pitch.

  1. Guard scans: lifeguard stays elevated with binoculars, not already in the water.
  2. Fast first response: a RescueArc comes off the rack instantly and is launched toward the victim.
  3. More than one shot: if the first misses or lands short, the guard fires again fast instead of burning time on one awkward throw.
  4. Float on impact: once armed and in water, the device inflates into high-visibility flotation meant to be grabbed or leaned onto.
  5. Swim-out second: the guard still enters if needed, but the victim gets help earlier.

Why this markets well

“Don’t make the first rescue move a long swim. Make it a better throw.”

This is the billion-dollar version of the story: not just a gadget, but a new standard rescue surface at every guarded water edge — beach stands, pool decks, docks, patrol boats, marinas, and waterfront homes.

Concept schematic

What has to exist for this to be real.

  • Throw body: grippy, football/vortex-like shell that is still soft enough to avoid turning into a dangerous projectile.
  • Arm / safe state: visual, idiot-resistant arming logic so it does not deploy by accident.
  • Compressed gas core: likely CO₂ puncture-based inflation for first serious prototype work.
  • Trigger path: water activation plus backup delay, not battery-pump fantasy as the primary system.
  • Inflated geometry: must be easier to grab than the compact throw body and visible in surf/chop.

Patent and IP reality

The broad idea is not enough. The wedge has to get narrower.

Closest prior art likely already covers parts of throwable inflatable flotation. The defensible claim area is narrower: superior throw form factor, safe deployment sequence, self-orienting flotation geometry, rackable rescue workflow, and measured human throw-performance gains over ring buoys.

The right sequence is still: prior art first, invention disclosure second, prototype architecture third, then any funding language.

Full roadmap

Website first for story. Proof next for reality.

This page is the concept weapon. The company only gets real when the proof stack gets built in order.

Phase 1

Invention definition

  • Write the invention disclosure.
  • Define 3 to 5 embodiments.
  • Freeze the exact rescue moment and buyer story.

Phase 2

IP triage

  • Run attorney-grade prior-art search.
  • Map claim wedges and obviousness threats.
  • Decide provisional utility vs design path.

Phase 3

Prototype architecture

  • Soft shell, cartridge, trigger, bladder, re-arm path.
  • Bench tests for no-fire / false-fire / partial inflation.
  • Generate first CAD and BOM assumptions.

Phase 4

Performance proof

  • Human throw study against ring buoy baseline.
  • Distance, accuracy, deployment time, victim usability.
  • Use this data as the marketing spine.

Phase 5

Manufacturing reality

  • Supplier outreach: inflatables, soft goods, CO₂ hardware, industrial design.
  • MOQ, compliance map, landed-cost model.
  • Kill the idea early if the economics are fake.

Phase 6

Go-to-market and capital

  • Lifeguard pilot or marina pilot first.
  • Waitlist / buyer interest before crowdfunding.
  • Kickstarter only after proof and prototype maturity.

What wins the room

The pitch is not “inflates in water.”

The pitch is that 100 people can throw this farther and more accurately than a ring buoy, and the rescue target gets flotation faster because the first move is a better projectile.

  • Distance proof
  • Accuracy proof
  • Impact safety proof
  • Victim-grab usability proof

No-BS risk list

What could kill it

  • Projectile is too hard and hurts victims.
  • Inflation path misfires or false-fires.
  • Inflated form is hard to grab in panic.
  • Prior art crowds the broad concept.
  • Compliance burden kills cost structure.
